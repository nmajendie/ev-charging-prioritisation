## Executive Summary

This analysis identifies priority UK local authorities for early-stage EV charging pilot deployments, focusing on areas with the highest likelihood of strong utilisation and scalable rollout.

Using publicly available data, I developed a simple decision framework combining two core drivers of public charging demand:

* EV adoption intensity (EVs per 1,000 residents)
* Structural need for public infrastructure (population density)

Results indicate that dense urban boroughs with relatively high EV penetration — particularly in London — represent the strongest near-term pilot opportunities. These areas combine constrained off-street parking with a growing EV base, increasing the probability of high charger utilisation.

The analysis also identifies a second tier of “emerging hotspots” where density is high but EV penetration remains moderate, suggesting strong medium-term growth potential.

---

## Decision Question

Which UK local authorities should be prioritised for initial EV charging pilots to maximise utilisation and generate scalable deployment insights?

---

## Analytical Approach

The prioritisation framework evaluates local authorities across two dimensions:

### 1. Demand Intensity

Measured as EV registrations per 1,000 residents, capturing relative adoption levels while controlling for population size.

### 2. Deployability Pressure

Measured using population density (people per km²), serving as a proxy for limited private parking and greater reliance on public charging.

These metrics were aggregated to Upper Tier Local Authority (UTLA) level using official geographic lookups and land area data.

A composite score was created using percentile rankings, with sensitivity testing applying higher weight to density to reflect utilisation risk.

---

## Key Findings

### Prime Pilot Markets (High EV + High Density)

Top candidates include:

* Kensington and Chelsea
* Westminster
* Barnet
* Kingston upon Thames

These areas combine strong EV adoption with constrained urban form, suggesting high likelihood of charger utilisation and valuable early operational insights.

### Emerging Hotspots (High Density, Moderate EV)

Examples include:

* Camden
* Hammersmith and Fulham
* Harrow
* Merton

These markets may offer strong medium-term growth potential as EV adoption continues to rise.

### Selective Deployment Areas (High EV, Lower Density)

Richmond upon Thames illustrates the importance of considering urban form alongside adoption. While EV ownership is high, lower density suggests greater availability of off-street parking, reducing reliance on public infrastructure.

---

## Strategic Implications

1. **Urban density is a key driver of utilisation risk**
   Population density proved to be a strong differentiator when weighting scenarios were tested.

2. **London provides a natural pilot environment**
   The combination of high density, high income, and supportive policy environments makes London boroughs ideal for early deployments.

3. **A phased rollout strategy is appropriate**
   Initial pilots should focus on prime markets, followed by expansion into emerging hotspots as demand accelerates.

---

## Risks and Limitations

* Population density is an imperfect proxy for parking availability; more granular parking or housing data would improve precision.
* EV registration data does not capture charging behaviour or trip patterns.
* Analysis assumes static administrative boundaries and does not account for future policy changes.

Despite these limitations, the framework provides a robust comparative view suitable for early-stage prioritisation.

---

## Recommended Next Steps

1. Conduct site-level feasibility assessments within top-ranked authorities.
2. Incorporate housing stock or parking data to refine demand estimates.
3. Validate assumptions through pilot deployments and utilisation monitoring.
4. Expand the framework to include commercial viability indicators such as income or traffic flows.

---

## Conclusion

This analysis demonstrates a simple but defensible approach to identifying high-potential EV charging pilot markets. By combining demand intensity with structural deployability constraints, the framework highlights dense urban authorities as the strongest initial deployment candidates while identifying emerging markets for future expansion.
